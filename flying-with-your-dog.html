Flying with your dog in Canada? Here's what you need to know

If you've ever tried to figure out how to fly with your dog in Canada, you've probably noticed one thing pretty quickly. The information online is all over the place. Between airline websites, forums, and conflicting advice, it can feel like you're piecing together a puzzle that never fully clicks. So instead of guessing, here's a real-world breakdown based on an actual cross-country flight with my dog, Pluto, and what you actually need to know before booking.

Choosing the Right Aircraft Matters More Than You Think

Source: Star Alliance Virtual

One of the biggest mistakes people make when booking a flight with a dog is ignoring the aircraft type. Not all planes are created equal when it comes to in-cabin pet travel, and the difference between a smooth trip and a stressful one often comes down to what you're flying on.

Regional jets like the Bombardier Dash 8 and the Bombardier CRJ 900 are generally much more pet-friendly than narrow or wide-body jets. These aircraft typically feature a two-by-two seating configuration, which means more floor width between rows. Critically, they tend to have less obstructive underseat structures — no thick metal bars running across the floor — which gives your carrier significantly more room to fit underneath the seat in front of you.

On larger aircraft like an A320 or Boeing 737, the underseat space is smaller, the floor supports can block a proper fit, and you may find yourself spending the whole flight holding the carrier on your lap or fighting to keep it from shifting into the aisle.

When booking, look for the aircraft type in the flight details before you confirm. Here's what made the Dash 8 and CRJ 900 work so well for us:

  • More width under the seat — the carrier fit without forcing it
  • Two-by-two seating means fewer neighbours, less chaos when Pluto shifted around
  • No metal crossbars obstructing the underseat floor space
  • Shorter flight times on most routes, which is better for your dog's comfort
  • Noticeably less stress overall — for both the dog and the owner

If you have the choice between aircraft on a given route, always choose the regional jet for a pet-friendly flight.

Your Dog's Size Is Not As Clear-Cut As You'd Expect

Every Canadian airline has a listed weight limit for in-cabin pets, but here's the reality: there is no universal standard, and enforcement is wildly inconsistent.

Air Canada, WestJet, and Porter Airlines each publish their own guidelines. Some state the pet-plus-carrier combined weight must be under 10 kg. Others specify the pet alone must not exceed a certain limit. And yet, in practice, how strictly this is applied tends to come down to the individual gate agent you encounter on the day of your flight.

Pluto sits right at the edge of most carriers' limits. We've flown without a single question asked. We've also had gate agents pull out a scale and weigh the carrier before boarding. You simply cannot predict which experience you'll have. Some things I noticed across multiple flights:

  • The same airline can apply the rules very differently depending on the airport and the agent
  • Smaller airports tend to be more relaxed about enforcement than major hubs
  • If your dog appears calm and the carrier looks professional, you're less likely to face scrutiny
  • Bringing documentation — vet records, vaccination proof — can help if challenged
  • Booking early morning or late evening flights tends to mean quieter gate areas and less pressure

My advice: always comply with the published rules as closely as you can, but understand that the experience may vary. Don't bank on a lenient gate agent, but don't catastrophize the process either. Know your dog's weight, know the airline's published limit, and be prepared to advocate calmly.

The Carrier You Choose Can Make or Break the Trip

Blurry photo of Pluto in his soft-sided carrier under the seats in front of us.

Forget hard-sided carriers for in-cabin travel. They rarely fit under the seat and they don't give your dog any room to shift or get comfortable during the flight. The only real option for in-cabin travel in Canada is an expandable soft-sided carrier.

The key feature to look for is the expandable panel — usually a zippered section at the back or sides that opens up to give your dog a bit more room once you're seated and the flight is underway. This lets you compress the carrier down to squeeze under the seat during boarding and taxi, then open it up a bit once cruising altitude is reached so your dog has more space to lie down properly.

When shopping for a carrier, pay attention to the published dimensions for your specific airline and aircraft type. Most carriers have a listed maximum size for in-cabin pets, typically around 43 cm × 33 cm × 20 cm, but this varies. Measure your carrier and compare it against the airline's listed underseat dimensions for the specific aircraft you're flying.

A few things that made Pluto's carrier work well:

  • Mesh panels on all sides for airflow — dogs overheat quickly in enclosed spaces
  • Padded, washable interior liner — accidents happen, be prepared
  • Dual zipper pulls on all openings — easier to manage one-handed at the gate
  • Luggage pass-through strap on the back to slide over a rolling suitcase handle
  • Expandable side panels that added roughly 8–10 cm of extra depth once opened

Get your dog comfortable in the carrier well before your trip. Leave it out at home for a few weeks with the door open, toss in a favourite blanket or toy, and let them treat it as a safe space rather than a travel stressor.

What Happens At The Airport and Onboard

Once you've booked your flight and prepared your carrier, the actual airport experience is more straightforward than most people expect. Here's what the process looks like from check-in to landing.

At check-in: Let the agent know you have an in-cabin pet. You'll pay the pet fee here ($50 CAD each way on most major Canadian carriers), and they'll add a pet notation to your booking. Keep your receipt. Some agents will attach a tag to your carrier — this is normal.

Through security: You'll be asked to remove your dog from the carrier and carry them through the metal detector in your arms while the carrier goes through the X-ray machine. Have your dog on a leash or harness before you reach the security lane. This is the moment most dogs get a bit anxious, so stay calm and move quickly.

At the gate: The gate agent may do a visual inspection of the carrier and ask to confirm your dog is inside. They may also weigh the carrier if they're strict that day. Arrive at the gate early so you have time to deal with any questions without the stress of boarding pressure.

Onboard: The flight attendant will confirm where your carrier should be stored — always under the seat in front of you, never in the overhead bin. You'll typically be asked to zip the carrier fully closed during takeoff and landing. Once at cruising altitude, most flight attendants are relaxed about the carrier zipper being opened slightly so your dog has more air circulation. Key rules to follow:

  • Dog stays in the carrier for the entire flight — no exceptions
  • Carrier must be fully closed and stored under the seat for takeoff and landing
  • Do not place the carrier on the seat beside you during boarding — it belongs under the seat
  • Keep your dog calm — bring familiar-smelling items and avoid feeding right before the flight
  • Water can be offered through the mesh with a travel dropper if the flight is long

Most flight attendants are understanding and even friendly about it. Being prepared, quiet, and organized goes a long way toward a smooth experience for everyone on board.

The Cost Is Simpler Than You'd Expect

Despite all the other variables involved in flying with a dog in Canada, the cost is refreshingly consistent. Most major Canadian airlines — Air Canada, WestJet, and Porter — charge $50 CAD each way for an in-cabin pet.

That's it. No hidden fees at the gate, no additional charge for the carrier, and no markup for certain routes or aircraft types. You pay at check-in and the fee is the same whether you're flying Toronto to Vancouver or Ottawa to Halifax.

A few things worth noting on cost:

  • The fee is per flight segment, not per trip — a connection means two fees
  • You cannot add the pet fee online with every airline — some require you to call ahead or add it at check-in
  • Budget carriers may have different policies or fees entirely — always check before booking
  • Pet fees are generally non-refundable if your travel plans change

Compared to cargo transport (which can run $150–$300+ each way depending on the dog's size), in-cabin is a significant bargain — and far less stressful for your dog.

The Bottom Line

Flying with your dog in Canada is absolutely doable — and once you've done it once, it feels far less daunting the second time. The process has real quirks and inconsistencies, but if you go in prepared, the experience tends to be smooth.

Here's the condensed version of everything you need to keep in mind:

  • Book the right aircraft. Regional jets like the Dash 8 and CRJ 900 are significantly more pet-friendly than narrow or wide-body jets. Check the aircraft type before confirming your booking.
  • Choose an expandable soft-sided carrier. Hard carriers won't fit. Get a quality soft-sided carrier with mesh ventilation and an expandable panel, and start acclimating your dog to it weeks before the trip.
  • Know the weight limits but expect inconsistency. Follow the published rules as closely as possible, but understand that enforcement varies by airport, by airline, and by the individual gate agent you get on the day.
  • Arrive early and stay calm. Extra time at the gate makes every step of the process easier. A calm owner makes for a calmer dog.
  • Budget $50 CAD each way. Pay at check-in, keep your receipt, and factor in each flight segment if you have a connection.
  • Prepare your dog, not just your bag. Familiar smells, a light meal before the trip (not right before boarding), and a practised routine with the carrier will do more than any checklist.

Pluto has now flown with me three times. Each trip has been a little easier. The fundamentals don't change — book smart, pack right, and go in with realistic expectations about how consistent (or not) the experience will be.
